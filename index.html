📊 Pool Snapshot

📖 Day 2 Recap — Friday, April 10

The Masters has a runaway leader. Rory McIlroy fired a bogey-free 65 — capped by four straight birdies to close — and now sits at 12-under, six shots clear of the field. It's the largest 36-hole lead in Masters history. He's chasing back-to-back green jackets, which only Nicklaus, Faldo, and Tiger have ever pulled off. Behind him, Day 2 was a meat grinder: the cut landed at +4, and one of the pre-tournament co-favorites just walked off Augusta in disbelief.

🟢 Heroes of the Day

Rory McIlroy (-12, 65) — Pure domination. After a slow middle stretch where Patrick Reed briefly pulled even, Rory closed birdie-birdie-birdie-birdie to break the tournament wide open. Six shots clear at the halfway mark — three shots better than any defending champion in Masters history at this point. The pool's most-picked primary is now the engine of nearly every contender in the standings.

Patrick Reed (-6, 67) — The biggest pre-tournament value pick keeps delivering. Reed actually pulled even with Rory through 12 before the leader's closing birdie barrage. He's solo T2 with Sam Burns, four shots clear of everyone else not named McIlroy. Mike Scarr and Simon Stolz remain the only two entries who picked Reed as a primary — though their fortunes diverge sharply (more on that below).

Tommy Fleetwood (-5, 68) — Two eagles in one round. Fleetwood eagled the par-5 8th and then dropped the eagle putt on 15 to vault into a tie for fourth alongside Justin Rose and Shane Lowry. Quiet Thursday, statement Friday.

Tyrrell Hatton (66) — Hit all 18 greens in regulation, becoming just the third player in the last 30 years to do so at Augusta (Furyk in '09, Na in '20). Not a pool pick anywhere, but a feat worth flagging.

Brooks Koepka (69) — Switched his driver setting from A1 to B1 between rounds and suddenly looks like Major Brooks again. Five-time major winner is firmly in the mix.

🔴 Disasters of the Day

Bryson DeChambeau — MISSED CUT. Devastating. After clawing back from his Round 1 mess with a birdie at 15 to reach +3 (inside the cut), Bryson stepped to the 18th tee needing only a bogey to play the weekend. He launched his drive into the woods, and walked off with a triple-bogey 7. Final: +5, just outside the cut. For pool entries with Bryson as a primary, his contribution is now frozen at his 36-hole score — no more improvement possible.

Jon Rahm — Made the cut on the number. Mixed news. After Thursday's catastrophic 78, Rahm shot 70 and birdied the 16th to land exactly on +4. He plays the weekend, but he's 16 shots back and effectively out of contention for the green jacket. The 13 entries with Rahm get to keep him, but only as deadweight.

Scottie Scheffler (74) — The world No. 1's quest for a third green jacket in five years is in serious trouble. Scheffler couldn't find anything on Friday and now sits well off the lead. Multiple long-running streaks (consecutive rounds at par or better, top-10 finishes at Augusta) all snapped or in jeopardy.

Akshay Bhatia — Heartbreak cut. Hit a gorgeous bunker shot at 17 to nearly hole out, then double-bogeyed 18 to fall to the wrong side of the line. Brutal.

🎯 The Pool Story

Debby Chan is leading the pool at $6.53M with the dream lineup: Aberg + Fleetwood + McIlroy + Rose. Three of her four picks sit in the top 6 on the Masters leaderboard, and she's the only entry combining McIlroy with both Fleetwood AND Rose. Her ~$580K cushion over 2nd place is built on a card where every pick made the cut — no dead weight, no MC frozen at $25K, no tiebreaker anxiety.

Cody Pantages sits 2nd at $5.95M with McIlroy + Rose + Schauffele + Scheffler. On paper this looks elite, but Schauffele only finished at -2 after bogeying Amen Corner (11–12–13), and Scheffler had the worst day of any made-cut pick (E, after a second-round 74). Those two underperforming prize slots — Schauffele buried in a 4-way tie at T16 and Scheffler stuck in a brutal 8-way tie at T24 — are why he's $580K behind Debby instead of pressuring her at the top.

The "McIlroy gravity" effect reshaped the top 10. McIlroy's prize of $4.5M is worth 2.3× the T2 prize of $1.98M — an enormous gap that pulls any entry containing him straight into the money. The casualty: Mike Scarr, whose contrarian Reed + Rose + Schauffele + Scheffler stack had him solo 6th under strokes scoring, now drops out of the top 10 entirely at ~$3.43M. Reed at T2 is excellent, but "excellent" isn't "$4.5M excellent." Meanwhile Scarr Don, Forbes Heather, Pearce Barb, Maitland Anthony, and Thomas Paul all jumped into the top 10 purely on the strength of McIlroy as a primary.

Rob Forbes's McIlroy-as-alt decision is now catastrophic. His four primaries (Aberg + Fleetwood + Schauffele + Young) are all alive and well, but under prize-money scoring the gap between McIlroy-as-primary ($4.5M) and McIlroy-as-alt ($0 contribution, pure tiebreaker) is the single biggest swing in the pool. Had he swapped any primary for McIlroy, he'd be pushing Debby for first. Instead he's outside the top 10 at ~$2.08M — and his $4.5M alt is the cruelest tiebreaker edge that will never be used.

Pearce Barb is top 10 at $5.14M despite losing DeChambeau to the cut. Her card — DeChambeau ($25K frozen) + Fitzpatrick ($262K) + McIlroy ($4.5M) + Schauffele ($349K) — shows the inversion this year's scoring has produced: McIlroy alone covers 88% of her total, and the MC'd DeChambeau barely dents her position. Under strokes scoring, a MC was a +5 anchor that sank you; under prize money, it's a $25K floor you hardly notice when your other picks are earning millions.

Scarr Ben is now dead last at $519K. His lineup — Aberg + DeChambeau + Fitzpatrick + Rahm — got hit twice: DeChambeau MC'd at $25K, and Rahm finished at +4 stuck in a brutal 8-way tie at T47 earning just $58K. Aberg ($174K, buried in that 8-way T24) and Fitzpatrick ($262K) can't drag him out of the basement. Interestingly, Colin Ball — who was dead last under strokes scoring with his 2/4 active card (Bhatia and MacIntyre both MC) — is actually ahead of Ben here at ~$1.04M, entirely because Fleetwood at $930K is doing 90% of Colin's work single-handedly.
